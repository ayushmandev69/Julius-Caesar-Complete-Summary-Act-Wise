I

Act I — The Crown Refused

Rome celebrates Caesar's triumph, a soothsayer warns of danger, and Cassius begins working on Brutus's loyalties.

Scene I

A street in Rome

Tribunes Flavius and Marullus scold a carpenter and a cobbler for skipping work to celebrate Caesar's victory over Pompey's sons. The tribunes are angry that the crowd once cheered for Pompey and now cheers for the man who defeated him. They send the commoners home and go off to strip decorations from Caesar's statues, hoping to clip his rising power before it grows too great.

Key Moments

  • Commoners abandon their trades to celebrate Caesar's triumph over Pompey's sons.
  • Marullus reminds the crowd they once worshipped Pompey the same way.
  • Flavius and Marullus resolve to "disrobe" Caesar's statues of festive trophies.
  • The scene plants the play's first warning: popularity is fickle, and unchecked power is dangerous.

Important Dialogue

"You blocks, you stones, you worse than senseless things!"Marullus, rebuking the fickle crowd
"These growing feathers pluck'd from Caesar's wing / Will make him fly an ordinary pitch."Flavius, on humbling Caesar's growing power

Scene II

A public place

Caesar processes through Rome with his entourage. A Soothsayer cries out a warning to "beware the ides of March," which Caesar dismisses. Left alone, Cassius begins to probe Brutus's feelings about Caesar's rising power, recalling a swimming contest in which he once saved Caesar's life, and arguing that Brutus is just as worthy of honour as Caesar. Shouts are heard offstage — Casca later reports that Antony offered Caesar a crown three times, which Caesar refused each time amid growing applause, before falling down in an apparent fit. Cassius begins plotting to draw Brutus into a conspiracy.

    • The Soothsayer's famous warning: "Beware the ides of March."
    • Cassius flatters and provokes Brutus, planting doubts about Caesar's ambition.
    • Cassius tells the story of saving Caesar from drowning in the Tiber, mocking Caesar's "godlike" reputation.
    • Casca reports Antony offered Caesar a crown three times; the crowd cheered when Caesar refused it each time.
    • Caesar collapses in the marketplace — Cassius calls it the "falling sickness," hinting at Caesar's human frailty.
    • Caesar privately tells Antony that he fears Cassius, who "thinks too much."
    • Cassius resolves, alone, to forge anonymous letters to manipulate Brutus into the conspiracy.

    Important Dialogue

    "Beware the ides of March."The Soothsayer, to Caesar
    "Men at some time are masters of their fates: / The fault, dear Brutus, is not in our stars, / But in ourselves, that we are underlings."Cassius, urging Brutus to act
    "Yond Cassius has a lean and hungry look; / He thinks too much: such men are dangerous."Caesar, to Antony
    "Well, Brutus, thou art noble; yet, I see, / Thy honourable metal may be wrought / From that it is disposed."Cassius, alone, plotting Brutus's manipulation

    Scene III

    A street — thunder and lightning

    A wild storm rages over Rome. Casca describes terrifying omens to Cicero — fire raining from the sky, a lion roaming the Capitol, men engulfed in flame. Cassius arrives and reinterprets these omens, arguing they are warnings against Caesar's tyranny rather than random chance. He reveals that the senate intends to crown Caesar king the next day and recruits Casca into the conspiracy. Cinna arrives with news that other conspirators wait at Pompey's porch, and Cassius sends him to plant forged letters where Brutus will find them, confident that Brutus's reputation will lend the conspiracy legitimacy.

    Key Moments

    • Unnatural omens during the storm — fire, a roaming lion, ghosts — set a mood of dread.
    • Cassius interprets the omens as signs against Caesar's ambition, not nature.
    • Cassius reveals the senate plans to crown Caesar king the next day.
    • Casca agrees to join the conspiracy against Caesar.
    • Cassius arranges for forged letters to be planted to persuade Brutus to join.

    Important Dialogue

    "Three parts of him / Is ours already, and the man entire / Upon the next encounter yields him ours."Cassius, on winning over Brutus

    II

    Act II — The Conspiracy Forms

    Brutus wrestles with his conscience, the conspirators meet under cover of night, and Calpurnia's nightmares foreshadow disaster.

    Scene I

    Rome — Brutus's orchard

    Alone before dawn, Brutus reasons that Caesar must die — not for who he is now, but for who he might become once crowned. He compares Caesar to a serpent's egg that should be destroyed before it hatches. Lucius brings him an anonymous letter (one of Cassius's forgeries) urging him to act for Rome's sake. The conspirators arrive in disguise, and Brutus agrees to lead them, though he insists there be no oath and that Mark Antony's life be spared, arguing they should be "sacrificers, not butchers." After the conspirators leave, Brutus's wife Portia begs to know the cause of his distress, and Caius Ligarius arrives, sick but willing to join any cause Brutus leads.

    Key Moments

    • Brutus's soliloquy: Caesar must be killed for what he might become, not for what he has done.
    • The forged letter convinces Brutus that "Rome" itself demands action.
    • The conspirators meet at Brutus's house in the dark, hiding their faces.
    • Brutus refuses to let the conspirators swear an oath, trusting their honour instead.
    • Brutus overrules Cassius and insists Antony's life be spared — a fatal mercy.
    • Portia pleads with Brutus to share his troubles; he promises to tell her soon.
    • The sick Caius Ligarius joins the conspiracy out of loyalty to Brutus.

    Important Dialogue

    "It must be by his death: and for my part, / I know no personal cause to spurn at him, / But for the general."Brutus, beginning his soliloquy
    "And therefore think him as a serpent's egg / Which, hatch'd, would, as his kind, grow mischievous, / And kill him in the shell."Brutus, justifying the assassination
    "Let's be sacrificers, but not butchers, Caius."Brutus, refusing to kill Antony
    "O ye gods, / Render me worthy of this noble wife!"Brutus, to Portia

    Scene II

    Caesar's house — thunder and lightning

    Calpurnia, shaken by nightmares and thrice crying out in her sleep, begs Caesar not to go to the Senate. Caesar initially refuses to be governed by fear, but after hearing that the augurers found no heart in a sacrificed beast, he agrees to stay home for Calpurnia's sake. Decius Brutus then arrives and cleverly reinterprets Calpurnia's dream of Caesar's bleeding statue as a favourable omen, while warning that the senate might mock Caesar as fearful if he stays away — and might change their minds about offering him the crown. Flattered and persuaded, Caesar decides to go to the Capitol after all, accompanied by the very men plotting his death.

    Key Moments

    • Calpurnia describes horrifying omens and pleads with Caesar to stay home.
    • Caesar declares that "cowards die many times before their deaths."
    • The augurers cannot find a heart in the sacrificial beast — an ill omen Caesar dismisses.
    • Decius Brutus reinterprets Calpurnia's nightmare as a sign of Rome's prosperity.
    • Decius warns that the senate may mock Caesar or rescind the crown if he stays away.
    • Caesar, flattered, changes his mind and agrees to go to the Capitol.
    • The conspirators arrive to escort Caesar, masking their intentions with friendly words.

    Important Dialogue

    "Cowards die many times before their deaths; / The valiant never taste of death but once."Caesar, to Calpurnia
    "Caesar shall forth: the things that threaten'd me / Ne'er look'd but on my back."Caesar, asserting his fearlessness
    "Your statue spouting blood in many pipes, / In which so many smiling Romans bathed, / Signifies that from you great Rome shall suck / Reviving blood."Decius Brutus, twisting Calpurnia's dream
    "That every like is not the same, O Caesar, / The heart of Brutus yearns to think upon!"Brutus, aside, hiding his guilt

    Scene III

    A street near the Capitol

    This brief scene shows Artemidorus, a minor soothsayer figure, reading aloud the warning letter he intends to hand to Caesar — naming every conspirator by name. He plans to intercept Caesar on the way to the Capitol, hoping the warning will save Caesar's life if read in time.

    Key Moments

    • Artemidorus's letter names all the conspirators directly to Caesar.
    • Creates dramatic irony: the audience knows Caesar has one last chance to be warned.

    Important Dialogue

    Scene IV

    A street before Brutus's house

    Portia, anxious and unable to keep still, sends Lucius to the Capitol for news, revealing how hard it is for her to keep her husband's secret. The Soothsayer passes by, intending to find a clear spot from which to warn Caesar personally. Left alone, Portia frets over "how weak a thing the heart of woman is" and prays for Brutus's success, underscoring the tension and helplessness felt by those outside the conspiracy.

    Key Moments

    • Portia's anxiety reveals the emotional cost of Brutus's secrecy.
    • The Soothsayer reappears, still hoping to warn Caesar in person.
    • Portia's fear builds suspense right before the assassination.

    Important Dialogue

    "I have a man's mind, but a woman's might. / How hard it is for women to keep counsel!"Portia

    III

    Act III — The Assassination

    Caesar falls at the Capitol, Brutus and Antony each address Rome, and the city descends into chaos and revenge.

    Scene I

    Rome, before the Capitol

    Caesar arrives at the Capitol, brushing past Artemidorus's warning letter. Metellus Cimber kneels to plead for his banished brother's return, drawing the other conspirators close under the guise of supporting his petition. As Caesar refuses, the conspirators surround him, and at Casca's signal — "Speak, hands for me!" — they stab him one by one. Seeing Brutus among his attackers, Caesar utters his last words and dies. The conspirators briefly celebrate, declaring "Liberty! Freedom!", but Antony, after sending word through a servant, arrives to mourn Caesar publicly while privately vowing revenge once he is alone with the body.

    Key Moments

    • Caesar ignores Artemidorus's letter, sealing his fate.
    • Metellus Cimber's petition is the conspirators' pretext to surround Caesar.
    • Casca strikes first; the conspirators stab Caesar in turn, Brutus last.
    • Caesar's dying words to Brutus mark the emotional climax of the betrayal.
    • Brutus insists the killers wash their hands in Caesar's blood as a symbol of justice, not murder.
    • Antony negotiates with the conspirators, securing permission to speak at Caesar's funeral.
    • Alone with the corpse, Antony privately swears revenge and predicts civil war.

    Important Dialogue

    "Et tu, Brute! Then fall, Caesar."Caesar, dying
    "Liberty! Freedom! Tyranny is dead!"Cinna, after the assassination
    "Let's kill him boldly, but not wrathfully; / Let's carve him as a dish fit for the gods."Brutus, before the murder
    "A curse shall light upon the limbs of men... / Cry 'Havoc,' and let slip the dogs of war."Antony, alone with Caesar's body

    Scene II

    The Forum

    Brutus addresses the citizens first, explaining in measured prose that he loved Caesar but loved Rome more, and killed him only because of his ambition. The crowd is won over and even wants to crown Brutus. He departs, asking the people to stay and hear Antony, whom he permits to speak under restriction. Antony then delivers his famous funeral oration — repeating "Brutus is an honourable man" with mounting irony, displaying Caesar's wounds, and revealing Caesar's generous will. The crowd's mood flips entirely: they turn into a violent mob bent on burning the conspirators' houses, forcing Brutus and Cassius to flee Rome.

    Key Moments

    • Brutus's prose speech justifies the killing as necessary for Rome's freedom.
    • The crowd initially supports Brutus and even wants to make him king.
    • Antony's funeral oration uses repetition and irony to turn the crowd against the conspirators.
    • Antony reveals Caesar's will, in which he leaves money and land to every citizen.
    • The mob erupts into violence, planning to burn the conspirators' homes.
    • Brutus and Cassius are forced to flee the city as Rome descends into chaos.

    Important Dialogue

    "Not that I loved Caesar less, but that I loved / Rome more."Brutus, to the citizens
    "Friends, Romans, countrymen, lend me your ears; / I come to bury Caesar, not to praise him."Antony, opening his funeral oration
    "For Brutus is an honourable man; / So are they all, all honourable men."Antony, repeating with growing irony
    "O judgment! thou art fled to brutish beasts, / And men have lost their reason."Antony, on the crowd's fickleness
    "Now let it work. Mischief, thou art afoot, / Take thou what course thou wilt!"Antony, after stirring the mob

    Scene III

    A street in Rome

    In a short, brutal scene, the mob encounters Cinna the poet — not Cinna the conspirator — and demands to know his name. Despite his pleas that he is "Cinna the poet," the crowd decides his name alone is reason enough to "tear him to pieces," illustrating how mob violence has spiralled out of all reason and control.

    Key Moments

    • The mob mistakes Cinna the poet for Cinna the conspirator.
    • The crowd attacks him anyway, showing the senseless cruelty unleashed by Antony's speech.
    • The scene marks the full collapse of civic order in Rome.

    IV

    Act IV — The Reckoning Begins

    Rome splits into two camps: the new triumvirate hunts down enemies, while Brutus and Cassius's friendship is tested by grief and suspicion.

    Scene I

    A house in Rome

    Antony, Octavius, and Lepidus now rule Rome as a triumvirate, coldly drawing up a death list of political enemies — Lepidus even agrees to sacrifice his own brother. After Lepidus leaves on an errand, Antony dismisses him to Octavius as a weak, replaceable tool, comparing him to a horse or a donkey used to carry burdens. The two then turn to the real threat: Brutus and Cassius are raising armies, and the triumvirs must prepare for war.

    Key Moments

    • The new triumvirate (Antony, Octavius, Lepidus) draws up a proscription list of enemies to be killed.
    • Lepidus consents to his own brother's death, showing the ruthlessness of the new regime.
    • Antony privately mocks Lepidus as a disposable underling, not a true equal.
    • Antony and Octavius pivot to plan their military campaign against Brutus and Cassius.

    Important Dialogue

    "This is a slight unmeritable man, / Meet to be sent on errands."Antony, on Lepidus
    "So is my horse, Octavius; and for that / I do appoint him store of provender."Antony, comparing Lepidus to an animal

    Scene II

    Camp near Sardis, before Brutus's tent

    Brutus's army awaits Cassius, whose lieutenant Lucilius reports a coolness in Cassius's welcome — a sign, Brutus notes, that their friendship may be fraying. When Cassius arrives, he immediately accuses Brutus of wronging him, and the two generals retire to Brutus's tent to argue privately, away from their soldiers' eyes.

    Key Moments

    • Lucilius hints that Cassius's loyalty to Brutus is cooling.
    • Brutus delivers a brief meditation on how "hollow men" fail under real pressure.
    • Cassius and Brutus's tension boils over in public before they retreat to argue privately.

    Important Dialogue

    "Thou hast described / A hot friend cooling."Brutus, on Cassius's changed manner
    "Most noble brother, you have done me wrong."Cassius, confronting Brutus

    Scene III

    Brutus's tent

    Brutus and Cassius quarrel bitterly — Brutus accuses Cassius of taking bribes and refusing him gold for his legions; Cassius, hurt, offers his dagger and bares his chest, daring Brutus to kill him if he truly believes him dishonourable. The two reconcile, and Brutus reveals that his wife Portia has died by swallowing fire in grief and fear. Messala arrives with news that Antony and Octavius have executed a hundred senators, including Cicero. Brutus and Cassius then debate strategy: against Cassius's advice to wait, Brutus insists they march to meet the enemy at Philippi immediately, arguing that "there is a tide in the affairs of men" that must be seized. That night, the ghost of Caesar appears to Brutus, warning that they will meet again at Philippi.

    Key Moments

    • Brutus and Cassius's quarrel over bribery and unpaid gold nearly ends their friendship.
    • Cassius offers his own dagger and bare chest, daring Brutus to kill him.
    • The two reconcile and share a cup of wine "to bury all unkindness."
    • Brutus reveals Portia's death by suicide, grieving with controlled stoicism.
    • News arrives that a hundred senators, including Cicero, have been executed.
    • Brutus overrules Cassius's caution and insists on marching to Philippi immediately.
    • The Ghost of Caesar appears to Brutus, foretelling their next meeting at Philippi.

    Important Dialogue

    "There is my dagger, / And here my naked breast; within, a heart / Dearer than Plutus' mine, richer than gold."Cassius, in despair during the quarrel
    "O Cassius, I am sick of many griefs."Brutus, before revealing Portia's death
    "There is a tide in the affairs of men, / Which, taken at the flood, leads on to fortune."Brutus, urging immediate action
    "Why comest thou? ... To tell thee thou shalt see me at Philippi."Brutus and the Ghost of Caesar

    V

    Act V — Philippi

    The two armies clash at Philippi. One by one, the conspirators meet their ends, and Rome's civil war reaches its tragic conclusion.

    Scene I

    The plains of Philippi

    The two armies meet for a tense parley before battle. Antony and Octavius exchange bitter insults with Brutus and Cassius, each side blaming the other for Rome's bloodshed. Before the fighting begins, Cassius — uncharacteristically superstitious — tells Messala of bad omens: eagles that once followed his army have been replaced by circling ravens and crows. Brutus and Cassius then share a solemn, possibly final farewell, each acknowledging that if the battle is lost, they may never meet again.

    Key Moments

    • A war of words between the two sides before battle — Antony and Octavius vs. Brutus and Cassius.
    • Cassius, normally a sceptic, admits to feeling unsettled by recent bad omens.
    • Brutus states his philosophical opposition to suicide, foreshadowing his later choice.
    • Brutus and Cassius exchange a moving, possibly final farewell before the battle.

    Important Dialogue

    "O, if thou wert the noblest of thy strain, / Young man, thou couldst not die more honourable."Brutus, to Octavius
    "For ever, and for ever, farewell, Cassius! / If we do meet again, why, we shall smile."Brutus, parting before battle

    Scene III

    Another part of the field

    Cassius, believing his side is losing and his friend Titinius captured, despairs. He orders his servant Pindarus to kill him with the very sword that helped slay Caesar, calling it poetic justice. Titinius returns moments later, having actually been celebrating a victory, and finds Cassius dead. Overcome with grief and guilt, Titinius takes his own life as well. Messala arrives to find both men dead and goes to deliver the tragic news to Brutus, who mourns Cassius as "the last of all the Romans."

    Key Moments

    • Cassius mistakenly believes Titinius has been captured by the enemy.
    • Cassius orders Pindarus to kill him with the sword that killed Caesar.
    • Titinius returns alive, having actually won his skirmish, and finds Cassius's body.
    • Grief-stricken, Titinius kills himself beside Cassius.
    • Brutus mourns Cassius as "the last of all the Romans."

    Important Dialogue

    "Caesar, thou art revenged, / Even with the sword that kill'd thee."Cassius, dying
    "O hateful error, melancholy's child."Messala, on Cassius's fatal mistake
    "The last of all the Romans, fare thee well!"Brutus, mourning Cassius

    Scene IV

    Another part of the field

    In the chaos of battle, young Cato is killed, and Lucilius pretends to be Brutus in order to protect the real Brutus from capture, offering himself to the enemy soldiers. When Antony arrives and recognizes that the captured man is Lucilius and not Brutus, he is impressed by the loyalty shown and orders that Lucilius be treated kindly and kept safe, preferring such men as friends rather than enemies.

    Key Moments

    • Young Cato dies bravely in battle, proclaiming his lineage.
    • Lucilius impersonates Brutus to protect him, offering himself to be captured instead.
    • Antony, recognizing the deception, admires Lucilius's loyalty and spares him.

    Scene V

    Another part of the field

    With defeat certain, Brutus asks several of his remaining friends, one by one, to help him die rather than be captured; each refuses out of love and respect. Brutus reveals that Caesar's ghost has visited him twice, confirming that his hour has come. Finally, Strato agrees to hold Brutus's sword steady while Brutus runs onto it, ending his own life. Antony and Octavius arrive at the battle's end; Antony delivers the play's final tribute, calling Brutus "the noblest Roman of them all" because he alone acted from genuine concern for the common good, not envy of Caesar. Octavius orders that Brutus be buried with full honours.

    Key Moments

    • Brutus asks several loyal friends to help him die; all refuse.
    • Brutus confirms Caesar's ghost has appeared to him twice, sealing his fate.
    • Strato holds the sword while Brutus runs upon it, ending his life.
    • Brutus's final words address Caesar directly, finding peace in death.
    • Antony's closing eulogy calls Brutus "the noblest Roman of them all."
    • Octavius orders an honourable burial for Brutus, closing the play.

    Important Dialogue

    "I shall have glory by this losing day / More than Octavius and Mark Antony / By this vile conquest shall attain unto."Brutus, before his death
    "Caesar, now be still: / I kill'd not thee with half so good a will."Brutus, dying
    "This was the noblest Roman of them all... / He only, in a general honest thought / And common good to all, made one of them."Antony, on Brutus
    "This was a man!"Octavius, closing tribute to Brutus
