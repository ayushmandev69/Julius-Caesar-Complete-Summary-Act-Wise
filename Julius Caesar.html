ACT I

Key Moments

The tribunes Flavius and Marullus confront a crowd of commoners who are taking a holiday to celebrate Julius Caesar's triumphant return to Rome after defeating Pompey's sons. The tribunes are angered by the crowd's fickleness, reminding them how they used to cheer just as loudly for Pompey. They disperse the crowd and decide to remove the celebratory decorations from Caesar's statues.

Important Dialogues

"You blocks, you stones, you worse than senseless things!
O you hard hearts, you cruel men of Rome,
Knew you not Pompey?"
— Marullus

Important Word Meanings

  • Mechanical: Working-class tradesmen or laborers.
  • Cobble: To mend shoes, but also used as a pun meaning to botch or mess up.
  • Tributaries: Captives who must pay a tribute or ransom to Rome.
  • Intermit: To hold back, delay, or prevent.

Key Moments

During the Feast of Lupercal, a Soothsayer warns Caesar to "Beware the ides of March," which Caesar dismisses. Cassius stays behind with Brutus and begins a long, manipulative conversation to gauge Brutus's feelings toward Caesar's growing power. Cassius argues that Caesar is just a mortal man, no better than them. Casca later returns and tells them that Mark Antony offered Caesar a king's crown three times, and though Caesar refused it, he seemed to want it. Caesar also suffered a seizure ("falling sickness") in the marketplace.

Important Dialogues

"Men at some time are masters of their fates:
The fault, dear Brutus, is not in our stars,
But in ourselves, that we are underlings."
— Cassius
"Yond Cassius has a lean and hungry look;
He thinks too much: such men are dangerous."
— Caesar

Important Word Meanings

  • Ides of March: March 15th in the Roman calendar.
  • Cogitations: Deep thoughts or contemplations.
  • Accoutred: Fully dressed and equipped.
  • Colossus: A gigantic statue; Cassius compares Caesar to the Colossus of Rhodes.
  • Falling sickness: Epilepsy.

Key Moments

A violent and terrifying storm rages in Rome, accompanied by supernatural omens (a slave with a burning but uninjured hand, a lion in the streets, an owl hooting at noon). Casca is terrified, but Cassius interprets these signs as heaven's warning against Caesar's tyrannical rise. Cassius boldly recruits Casca into the conspiracy. They plan to leave forged letters for Brutus to ensure he joins their plot.

Important Dialogues

"Cassius from bondage will deliver Cassius:
Therein, ye gods, you make the weak most strong..."
— Cassius

Important Word Meanings

  • Prodigies: Omens, extraordinary events indicating impending disaster.
  • Portentous: Ominous; foreboding.
  • Thews: Muscular strength or physical power.
  • Factious: Active in a political group or faction (specifically, rebellious).

ACT II

Key Moments

Unable to sleep, Brutus paces his orchard and rationalizes that Caesar must be killed to prevent him from becoming a tyrant ("think him as a serpent's egg"). The conspirators arrive in the night to finalize their plans. Brutus convinces them not to swear an oath and argues against killing Mark Antony, claiming it would make their cause too bloody. Later, Portia, Brutus's wife, begs him to share his secrets, proving her strength by revealing a self-inflicted wound on her thigh.

Important Dialogues

"And therefore think him as a serpent's egg
Which, hatch'd, would, as his kind, grow mischievous,
And kill him in the shell."
— Brutus
"Let's carve him as a dish fit for the gods,
Not hew him as a carcass fit for hounds"
— Brutus

Important Word Meanings

  • Adder: A venomous snake.
  • Phantasma: A hallucination or nightmare.
  • Erebus: The dark region of the underworld in Greek mythology.
  • Cautelous: Deceitful or overly cautious.

Key Moments

The morning of March 15th. Calpurnia has dreamt of Caesar's statue spouting blood, with Romans bathing their hands in it. She begs him not to go to the Capitol. The augurs (priests) also advise him to stay home after finding no heart in a sacrificed beast. Caesar initially agrees to stay home to appease his wife. However, Decius Brutus arrives and cleverly reinterprets the dream as a positive vision of Caesar providing sustaining blood to Rome. He manipulates Caesar's pride, suggesting the Senate might change their minds about offering him a crown if he cowers at home.

Important Dialogues

"Cowards die many times before their deaths;
The valiant never taste of death but once."
— Caesar

Important Word Meanings

  • Augurers: Priests who interpret the will of the gods through omens.
  • Statua: Statue.
  • Portents: Things that foreshadow a coming event; omens.
  • Cognizance: A badge or identifying mark.

Key Moments

Artemidorus reads aloud a letter he has written to Caesar. The letter explicitly names the conspirators (Brutus, Cassius, Casca, Cinna, Trebonius, Metellus Cimber, Decius Brutus, Caius Ligarius) and warns Caesar to trust none of them. He plans to hand this warning to Caesar as he passes by on his way to the Capitol.

Important Dialogues

"If thou read this, O Caesar, thou mayst live;
If not, the Fates with traitors do contrive."
— Artemidorus

Important Word Meanings

  • Emulation: Jealousy or envious rivalry.
  • Suitor: One who petitions or requests a favor from someone in power.

Key Moments

Portia is highly anxious, knowing her husband's terrible secret. She frantically sends the boy Lucius to the Capitol to observe Brutus and Caesar, though she struggles to give him a clear excuse for why he is going. The Soothsayer passes by, mentioning he is going to find a clear spot to try and warn Caesar once more.

Important Dialogues

"O constancy, be strong upon my side,
Set a huge mountain 'tween my heart and tongue!
I have a man's mind, but a woman's might."
— Portia

Important Word Meanings

  • Constancy: Firmness of mind; resolve.
  • Fray: A noisy fight or battle.

ACT III

Key Moments

The climax of the play. Caesar arrogantly brushes off Artemidorus's letter. Inside the Capitol, Trebonius draws Antony away. Metellus Cimber distracts Caesar by pleading for his banished brother. The other conspirators crowd around. Casca strikes first, and the others follow. Brutus strikes the final blow. Caesar falls, uttering "Et tu, Brute?". The conspirators bathe their hands in Caesar's blood. Antony returns, outwardly pledges peace with the assassins, but secretly requests to speak at Caesar's funeral to incite the mob.

Important Dialogues

"Et tu, Brute! Then fall, Caesar."
— Caesar
"O, pardon me, thou bleeding piece of earth,
That I am meek and gentle with these butchers!"
— Antony

Important Word Meanings

  • Puissant: Powerful; mighty.
  • Enfranchisement: Restoration of rights or freedom.
  • Lethe: River of forgetfulness/death; here representing Caesar's lifeblood.
  • Havoc: A military cry ordering soldiers to slaughter without mercy.

Key Moments

Brutus speaks to the plebeians, explaining logically that he killed Caesar not out of hatred, but because of Caesar's ambition, declaring, "Not that I loved Caesar less, but that I loved Rome more." The crowd agrees. However, Brutus leaves, allowing Mark Antony to speak. Using powerful rhetoric and bitter sarcasm ("Brutus is an honourable man"), Antony systematically tears apart Brutus's logic. He shows them Caesar's wounded body and reads Caesar's will (leaving money and land to the people). The crowd goes wild, forming a murderous mob against the conspirators.

Important Dialogues

"Not that I loved Caesar less, but that I loved Rome more."
— Brutus
"Friends, Romans, countrymen, lend me your ears;
I come to bury Caesar, not to praise him."
— Antony

Important Word Meanings

  • Censure: To judge or condemn.
  • Extenuated: Diminished, lessened, or underrated.
  • Testament: A will.
  • Mantle: A cloak.

Key Moments

An innocent poet named Cinna is accosted by the enraged plebeian mob. Even after he clarifies that he is Cinna the Poet, not Cinna the conspirator, the bloodthirsty crowd attacks him anyway ("Tear him for his bad verses"), showcasing the complete descent of Rome into violent anarchy.

Important Dialogues

"I am Cinna the poet, I am Cinna the poet...
Tear him for his bad verses, tear him for his bad verses."
— Cinna & The Fourth Citizen

Important Word Meanings

  • Fantasy: Imagination or dreams (as in Cinna's ill-boding dream).
  • Directly: Straightforwardly and without delay.

ACT IV

Key Moments

The Second Triumvirate (Antony, Octavius, and Lepidus) has taken power. They sit cold-bloodedly checking off a list of political enemies (including their own relatives) who must die. Antony compares Lepidus to an animal meant to carry loads, suggesting they will use him and then cast him aside. They plan to raise an army to fight Brutus and Cassius.

Important Dialogues

"He shall but bear them as the ass bears gold,
To groan and sweat under the business..."
— Antony (about Lepidus)

Important Word Meanings

  • Proscription: A decree condemning a person to death or banishment.
  • Provender: Dry food, such as hay or oats, for livestock.
  • Corporal: Physical; of the human body.

Key Moments

Outside Brutus's tent. Tension has grown between Brutus and Cassius. Brutus remarks to his servant Lucilius that Cassius is acting like a "hot friend cooling"—their relationship is becoming strained and formal. Cassius arrives, and the two agree to move into the tent to argue privately so their armies do not see their disunity.

Important Dialogues

"Thou hast described A hot friend cooling: ever note, Lucilius,
When love begins to sicken and decay,
It useth an enforced ceremony."
— Brutus

Important Word Meanings

  • Resolved: Informed or satisfied.
  • Jades: Worthless or worn-out horses.

Key Moments

A massive argument erupts. Brutus accuses Cassius of taking bribes ("an itching palm") and reminds him they killed Caesar for justice, not corruption. They reconcile after a bitter exchange. Brutus then reveals he is grieving: Portia, his wife, has killed herself by swallowing hot coals. Later, they discuss military strategy. Brutus overrides Cassius's cautious advice, deciding to march to Philippi to meet Antony and Octavius. Once alone, Brutus is visited by the Ghost of Caesar, who warns him they will meet at Philippi.

Important Dialogues

"There is a tide in the affairs of men,
Which, taken at the flood, leads on to fortune..."
— Brutus
"To tell thee thou shalt see me at Philippi."
— Ghost of Caesar

Important Word Meanings

  • Itching palm: A desire for money; greed.
  • Choler: Anger, irritability.
  • Drachmas: Silver coins of ancient Greece.
  • Niggard: To supply sparingly; to hoard.
  • Mace: A heavy club, signifying sleep weighing heavily on the boy Lucius.

ACT V

Key Moments

The rival armies face off. Before fighting, the leaders (Antony and Octavius vs. Brutus and Cassius) meet in the center of the battlefield to hurl insults at one another. After the parley fails, Cassius confides to Messala that today is his birthday and that he has seen bad omens (ravens and crows circling). Brutus and Cassius say a final, touching farewell to each other in case they die in battle.

Important Dialogues

"For ever, and for ever, farewell, Cassius!
If we do meet again, why, we shall smile;
If not, why then, this parting was well made."
— Brutus

Important Word Meanings

  • Exigent: A critical or urgent moment.
  • Parley: A discussion or conference between opposing sides.
  • Epicurus: Greek philosopher who taught that omens and the supernatural did not interfere with human life (Cassius used to believe this).

Key Moments

In a very brief scene, Brutus sends Messala with orders ("bills") to launch a sudden attack on Octavius's wing, perceiving weakness in their ranks. Brutus acts prematurely, leading to disastrous consequences for Cassius's side.

Important Dialogues

"Let them set on at once; for I perceive
But cold demeanor in Octavius' wing..."
— Brutus

Important Word Meanings

  • Bills: Written orders or dispatches.
  • Alarum: A call to arms or battle cry.

Key Moments

Cassius's troops are fleeing from Antony. Cassius sends Titinius to check on troops nearby. Pindarus, watching from a hill, misinterprets the scene and tells Cassius that Titinius has been captured. In despair, Cassius orders Pindarus to stab him with the very sword used to kill Caesar. He dies. Titinius returns (he was actually celebrating with Brutus's troops) and, finding Cassius dead, kills himself in grief. Brutus arrives, laments the power of Caesar's spirit, and prepares for a second fight.

Important Dialogues

"Caesar, thou art revenged,
Even with the sword that kill'd thee."
— Cassius
"O Julius Caesar, thou art mighty yet!
Thy spirit walks abroad and turns our swords
In our own proper entrails."
— Brutus

Important Word Meanings

  • Ensign: A flag or standard-bearer.
  • Melancholy: A gloomy state of mind; severe depression.
  • Apace: Swiftly; quickly.

Key Moments

The battle continues. Lucilius heroically pretends to be Brutus to draw attention away from the real Brutus. He is captured by Antony's soldiers. Antony recognizes him, appreciates his loyalty, and orders his men to treat Lucilius kindly, stating he would rather have such men as friends than enemies.

Important Dialogues

"Safe, Antony; Brutus is safe enough:
I dare assure thee that no enemy
Shall ever take alive the noble Brutus..."
— Lucilius

Important Word Meanings

  • Yield: To surrender.
  • Prize: A captive of high value.

Key Moments

Defeated, Brutus asks his remaining friends to help him kill himself. Clitus, Dardanius, and Volumnius refuse. Finally, Strato agrees to hold Brutus's sword while Brutus runs upon it. Brutus dies, stating his will to kill himself was stronger than his will to kill Caesar. Antony and Octavius find his body. Antony delivers a final eulogy, declaring Brutus "the noblest Roman of them all," because unlike the other conspirators who acted out of envy, Brutus acted out of genuine concern for the common good. Octavius orders a respectful burial for Brutus.

Important Dialogues

"Caesar, now be still:
I kill'd not thee with half so good a will."
— Brutus
"This was the noblest Roman of them all:
All the conspirators save only he
Did that they did in envy of great Caesar..."
— Antony

Important Word Meanings

  • Smatch: A taste or a smack; a slight quality of something.
  • Elements: The humors or constitutional nature of a person.
  • Tarry: To wait or stay in place.
